I am a Ph.D. student in the Department of Biological Systems Engineering at Virginia Tech and a member of the Sustainable & Intelligent Seafood Bioprocessing Laboratory.

A single question runs through my research: how can we improve seafood health, quality, and sustainability without increasing environmental costs?

I approach this challenge from both ends of the supply chain. On the production side, I develop PLGA-based microparticles that deliver probiotics and inactivated pathogens through the digestive tract of fish and shrimp, enhancing immunity and reducing disease-related losses in aquaculture. On the preservation side, my work focuses on two complementary directions. First, I develop bio-based passive radiative cooling materials that reduce temperature without electricity, providing sustainable solutions for energy-efficient cold chains. Second, I investigate the physiological, biochemical, and flavor-related changes that occur during seafood processing and storage, particularly in shellfish, to better understand the mechanisms underlying quality deterioration and guide the development of effective preservation strategies.

This focus grew out of earlier work on how aquatic animals respond to their environment. During my master's, I studied microplastic contamination in oysters and mussels. Taken together, these projects reflect what I care about most: building materials and systems that make seafood safer, fresher, and more sustainable—from the farm to the plate.
